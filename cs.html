Building the website was one story, and actually reaching out to an audience was another. The latter is also an important part, but far beyond the scope of engineering and design.

This is my first design project. Notably, it was my first coding project as well. I did not learn computer science in high school. When I first came across computer science in college and immediately became interested in it, I suddenly wondered why I had not known it earlier. That tiny curiosity soon grew stronger and stronger. In fall 2016, I did an independent study with Prof. Ryan Shaw in the School of Information and Library Science to study CS education in U.S. and Chinese high schools. Then, after all the research and literature review, I had some interesting insights and wrote a paper. Yet after that, I was like, “So what?”

The website was an attempt to answer the “so what” question. Few people like reading academic papers, so I wanted to present my research in a different, hopefully more engaging and personalized way. I wanted the website to have a “low floor and high ceiling”: it should be easily to explore in order to reach a boarder audience, yet it should also offer more content for those who want to know more. Those were my design challenges.

The Initial Design

To meet the "low floor and high ceiling" requirement, I had the "read more" and "show less" feature for all my content. Based on the website's topic, I also wanted to create more personalized contents based on the users nationality (U.S., China) and occupation (teacher, student, policy maker, other). I was influenced by the concept of "action journalism": action journalism seeks to not only provide readers with information, but also enable users to take actions based on the information. So I seeked to provide ways for different people to get involved in high school CS education. The initial wireframe was as follows:

I wanted the "How can I get involved" bar at the footer to stand out, so I chose a contrasting background color. After the user clicks on the orange bar, the page will ask their nationality and occupation, and then it will display relevant content.

Poster as Prototype

The next step would be user testing. Right around that time, I got a chance to present a poster at my university's Celebration of Undergraduate Research event. There were a lot of firsts involved in this project: it was my first research poster presentation as well! I suddenly thought, "Why not use this chance to do some user testing?" So I designed my poster as a paper prototype for my website.

If someone would ask about the "How can I get involved" part of the poster, that would be equivalent as clicking the orange bar on the website. I would then hand out a prepared flyer, which equaled the next page on the website. On the presentation day, many stopped by, but very few asked about "How can I get involved."

The tricky part would be to figure out why. I asked for some help from my professors. One suggested that it wasn't apparent that the orange bar was clickable/actionable. Besides, people often ignore footers. It wasn't enough to use a contrasting background color. It was better for the part to stand out as a clearly clickable call-to-action button.

The Re-design

Engineers are often told to not reinvent the wheel. I came to learn that the same holds true for designers as well. For the next few days, I looked at political campaign websites and studied their call-to-action buttons. I then redesigned my website as follows:

Now the orignal orange footer bar appeared as a item on the side bar menu. There were other reasons that I transformed the whole menu from a horizontal bar to a side bar. First, it could be hard to scan accross many items horizontally. Looking Back

I have said that the website was an attempt to answer the “so what” question. Looking back, was the attempt successful? Frankly, no. Building the website was one story, and actually reaching out to an audience was another. The latter is also an important part, but far beyond the scope of engineering and design. It is much like the classical question asked in entrepreneurship, “If you build it, will they come?” The answer will not always be clear from the beginning. Yet since then, I came to think of research as a form of entrepreneurship.
